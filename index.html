Katherine Geist

Genomics of social conflict & cooperation

I am in the final year of my PhD in the laboratory of Joan Strassmann and David Queller at Washington University in St. Louis in the Evolution, Ecology, and Population Biology program. I study genetic conflicts that can arise during sexual reproduction in seed plants, as well as during the asexual cycle of the social amoebae D. discoideum. To do this, I use a variety of tools, in particular bioinformatics, genomics, molecular evolution, and experimental evolution. I am particularly interested in conflict that can occur among genes, either within the same individualâ€™s genome or between the genomes of other individuals.

Cooperation is fundamental to the organization of life, yet for Darwinian selection it presents a problem: how can traits that aid others evolve and persist? Not only do individuals cooperate among themselves to form complex societies, but our bodies could not live without cooperation among cells or among the genes in the genome. My PhD Lab studies the evolution of the most extreme form of cooperation -- altruism -- in the social amoebae Dictyostelium discoideum and other organisms. They also study the cooperation and conflict between D. discoideum and its bacterial symbionts.
